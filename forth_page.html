A Netflix for manga?

Manga is often compared with anime, its sister industry, since their overseas fandoms have both risen from scanlators and fansubbers. While scanlators translate, edit and distribute manga, fansubbers do the same but with anime. Fansubbers started way back in the 1970s with a number of passionate anime fans who import untranslated VHS video tapes from Japan and circulate their translated versions among US anime fan clubs by mail. Due to the rise of the internet, the fansubbing community (like the scanlation one) became a huge empire in the 1990s, with thousands of groups competing to release translated versions of anime as soon as possible.

The fansubbing community is now history since “distribution in the industry has gotten so good at it” according to BlackAnon, an ex-scanlator and now a translator for two of the biggest anime localizers and streaming services in the US, Sentai filmworks and Funimation. At the moment, all of the latest anime shows are streamed in English on the same day or the same time on a platform with affordable monthly subscriptions, or even for free.

However, this hasn’t yet happened to the manga industry yet. The scanlation community is still going strong and its conflict with publishers is getting more intense. Lillian Diaz-Przybyl, a freelance editor for various manga publishers in the US and a former senior editor of TokyoPop, one of the biggest English manga publishers in the 2000s, has witnessed many attempts to solve this problem since she left TokyoPop in 2011. But as she says, “there isn’t really a final answer. Like, we’re still working on this. It’s still a work in progress 10 years later”.

Subscription chaos

Online manga is sold in weird ways. Different publishers have their own way to sell their manga online and within a publishers, different titles can be also sold differently even in the same online store. Shueisha, the biggest manga publisher in Japan publishes its titles in English through Viz, its US branch. Half of Viz’s digital titles belong to Weekly Shonen Jump magazine — the most circulated magazine in Japan — and are published through its own app that allow people to read them by paying a two-dollar-monthly-subscription-fee. The other half are either published via Shueisha’s new app which allows people to read their three first or three latest chapters for free or via other e-book distributors in which people only can purchase by volume.

Comixology, a subsidiary of Amazon that specializes in comics manages to get all of digital manga titles published in English on their platform but cannot get them in its “unlimited service” which lets people read all titles for a six-dollar-monthly-subscription-fee. Three of the four biggest manga publishers in the US: Viz Media, Yen Press, Kodansha USA all of them belong to Japanese publishers.. However, Kodansha USA has around one third of around 200 titles on this service; for other titles, readers still have to pay by volume. Also, even for Kodansha USA’s titles in unlimited service of Comixology, only their limited chapters are available to the subscribers. As if that wasn’t bizarre enough, those chapters that are unavailable for Comixology subscribers, are available for other platforms’ subscribers. “I think we’re at a time of chaos right now. It’s very hard to make money off online content” says Shannon Garrity, a manga editor, cartoonist and US comic market consultant with 20 years of experience.

How four biggest English manga publishers: Viz Media, Kodansha USA, Yen Press and Seven Seas publish their titles online.

All publishers prefer selling their manga titles by volume on Amazon/Comixology rather than by chapters on the same day or after a short time they are published in Japan. Publishers also seems not to like their titles published on subscription services, especially if those services were not owned by them.

Japanese manga publishers are supposed to compete with each other by nature. In Japan, manga is introduced to readers chapter by chapter in manga magazines. When those chapters are collected into a book, it is published under the brand of the magazine. Bookstores usually sort manga not just by subject matter or author but by publisher as well. Within publisher sections, they sort the manga according to the magazine. The brand identity of manga is so important that there are unwritten rules about which title to put next to another.

Publishers came together to create a Netflix for manga once: JManga. It made its debut in 2011 in San Diego at Comic-Con International, the oldest and biggest event for comic fans. Backed by Digital Comic Association’s members, JManga was be able to publish titles from 39 Japanese manga publishers on one portal. However, even though JManga managed to put dozens of publishers under one big umbrella, it could not do anything to unify how they operated. As a result, the payment structure of the platform was too complicated. “Reading on JManga was awful, people hated it”, remembers Professor June M. Mandeley, University of New Brunswick, Canada who has studied manga readers since 2009. JManga was shut down a year and a half after its launch.

It was so uncommon for Japanese publishers to work together that even in English-speaking markets, Japanese publishers preferred to use their own branch to handle their own titles, than associating with an independent publisher. “If there were a world that you can download an app that all of the different manga magazine kind of line up next to each other […] That’s I think it would make sense to people on kind of global level but I just don’t know how you could make that happens” – said Diaz-Przybyl.

But even if that world as imagined by Diaz-Przybyl existed, how to simultaneously publish thousands of titles from hundreds of manga magazines is another huge challenge, in part because it’s different from anime, “manga is still a very analog industry, people are still doing a lot of their drawing by hand and hand paper pages to their publishers” says Diaz-Przybyl. That said, all the texts in manga are manually pasted or drawn directly on rather than digitally laid out on a separate layer to the artwork. Behind any translated page of manga could be dozens of hours of typesetting and redrawing labor. If every single manga magazine in Japan had everything translated, it would be financially unviable as publishers cannot afford an in-house localization team to have every title translated on the same day it’s released in Japan.

Even if publishers managed to overcome the two obstacles above, they would still have to come up with a reader-friendly platform. Like many companies, publishers tend to make applications for themselves first rather than for fans who ironically have been so familiar with the user-centric designed aggregator created by scanlation community. Mandeley says people should have found the answers in piracy “…smart people would have made their platform be like those scans but they didn’t do that”

Ideas from pirates

In 2009, Taylor Matthews and Christopher Carter decided to build a bridge between scanlators and manga industry. They are the founders of MangaHelpers, the most influential scanlation forum community with 100, 000 unique visitors every day. They were confident that through MangaHelpers, they could build a digital publishing platform for Japanese publishers and scanlators and put out manga chapters on the same day as they were published in Japan. This would satisfy millions of readers who resorted to pirated content since they had to wait too long for the official English-language releases of their favorite titles. Matthews and Carter eventually had a meeting with Shueisha, the biggest manga publishers in Japan, after nearly four years of emailing back and forth. However, Shueisha refused their offer, saying they didn’t want to work with pirates in any form.

While Matthews moved off in a different direction, Carter has not yet given up on changing the publishers’ minds. The way to solve the ever-going conflict between scanlators and publishers “isn’t how do you eliminate piracy. The question is how do you make piracy unnecessary or inconvenience than just using it normally. Then the answer is to make the most convenient product in the world”, says Carter.

Carter is currently working on a digital publishing platform – GlobalComix that aims to be convenient for both readers and publishers. He is confident in the platform’s user experience not only because he has 10-year experience as a product manager for various technological companies and was part of MangaHelpers’ success. He also started GlobalComix by building a close community of just 100 independent comic creators and readers to understand their needs. Carter is in touch with them pretty much every day on Discord to get feedback and suggestions for the new features or new events for his platform.

With a free account, a publisher can upload an unlimited amount of pages of any size and has absolute control of the paywall, and readers’ forum for each of their titles on his site. The best thing is, they also can see their readers’ analytics. At the moment, there is no platform outside GlobalComix provide readers’ data for its publishers. “When we talk with people and we say, well here’s what we’re doing and they’re like ‘There’s no way that’s real!’”, says Carter.

Data is the secret weapon that Carter believes will eventually make even the most conservative manga publisher open up to international audiences and drop their stand-alone brand identity and fear of piracy. GlobalComix not only gives publishers data about their titles’ current audience though, it also suggests to publishers how to reach a new market with statistics of potential audiences who are reading other titles in a different language but which is similar to the publisher’s content.

GlobalComix can get a bigger picture of comic markets is because it isn’t restricted to manga but deals in all kind of comics. Manga is not the only kind of comic that has problems going global. Growing up in Norway where people of all ages read comics, Carter couldn’t read his favorite titles when he moved to New York for work. His platform’s ultimate goal is to let people anywhere in the world read any comic in their own language.

Here then is where the scanlation community comes in, to localize that many comics. Carter is building a localization process on GlobalComix that is a “marketplace which would function in much the same way as what a scanlation system does”, as Carter said. That means a scanlator or scanlation group who wants to become legitimate could propose to a publisher the titles they’d like to translate and a publisher can choose who they want to work with to localize their manga on the platform.

Carter wouldn’t have thought this was feasible if he hadn’t been involved with scanlators before. He believes that if there is ever to be a Netflix-like platform of manga, it will be based on the scanlation community. “Scanlation and piracy in general is like, the best content delivery system humanity created. It’s like the most efficient way for content to travel from country to country” says Nadav Hertzshtark, the CEO and co-founder of Fat Ginger Cat, an Israeli start-up that helps scanlators to become legitimate. Fat Ginger Cat has 2500 titles in its catalogue, which are provided by Medibang and Yaku-man, two Japanese companies that are both manga wholesalers and manga publishers. Fat Ginger Cat gives out those titles to scanlators to translate and then publishes them on their reading platform.

Hertzshtark and his team spent months on scanlation forums and scanlation groups after setting up his company, to introduce his start-up to the fandom. People who applied to his company were promised total freedom to choose what to translate from the available titles. They could also suggest other titles for Fat Ginger Cat to license. They were given no deadline. From there, Fat Ginger Cat’s in-house design team took care of the page layouts but it was the freelance translator who had the final say on how the pages looked.

Fat Ginger Cat has tried to replicate the freedom of the scanlation community on its platform. It trusts these fans to curate and edit content despite the risk that the project could be abandoned at any time: “They are just very good at what they are doing so let’s just give them a platform to do it and there’s no reason to try and replace them” says Hertzshtark. He won’t say how many fan translators are working on his platform. However, he says he currently has more than enough and is not taking any more of them on at the moment. In the future, he wants to let scanlators take over the layout as well.

Mobilizing the scanlation community

When Crunchyroll, a host for fansubbing anime became legitimate, it was helped by an army of fansubbers to translate a huge number of anime episodes on the same day as they were released in Japan. According to a recent video by an anime journalist, Callum May, when the fansubbers were first paid for their work, they were happy enough to accept any amount of money, leaving a bad “legacy” for the current generation of translators who aren’t able to live from their work as Crunchyroll translator. Crunchyroll has now became the most popular anime streaming platform but the rate of pay for the translators is the same or even lower than 10 years ago.

Scanlators have witnessed the mistake that the fansubbing community made. They have also seen how translators, typesetters and editors are treated in the manga industry, especially in digital publishing. People usually get no credit for digital-only title work. No matter how much the work differs from title to title, they often receive the same payment upfront even though the amount of money varies a lot among publishers. “Work in manga is not linear. Some pages can take seconds, some take minutes. Why should someone be paid $x for something that takes 20 minutes and the same $x for something that take two hours?” says GGpx, the head of Illuminati-manga, one of the oldest scanlation groups. The majority of scanlators would rather scanlate for free with no strings attached as a hobbyist than work for a company and be treated unfairly.

Attracting the best talents in the scanlation community to work on a big digital publishing platform will require a fairer payment model. However, all current payment models have disadvantages. Localization teams for languages that have small audiences would be paid much less compared to those working with popular languages if they are paid by word count, page count paying model. Those who have marketing skills would receive more than who do not if publishers go by revenue share model. Carter says he can at least make the best of the existing payment model with technology. When more and more publishers are using his platform, his machine learning software can predict an average market rate of translation from Japanese to English so the publisher cannot pay translator lower than that. Also, parts of the localization process like typesetting for example could be simplified and automated with technology. At the moment, there can be up to hours of labor needed to produce one manga page. That includes removing all the text, redrawing the missing artwork if you have to erase the text outside the speech bubbles, typing and styling the translation on the page. Both Carter and Hertzshtark are developing technology that would automatically remove and replace the text in speech bubbles, which means the typesetters would earn the same for less work. In the future, their technology will assist typesetters with redrawing as well. Freelancers who work on the platform will have their own profile and portfolio page, which will mean they are credited in every piece of work that they do.

***

Although they have promising platforms, GlobalComix and Fat Ginger Cat are still well aware that it will not be easy for upcoming, small tech startups like theirs to get all the content from manga publishers that they need to make their monthly subscription model viable. According to Hertshtark, only very big companies like Amazon are able to speak directly with publishers. Fat Ginger Cat can only work with wholesalers who have rights to distribute not-so-popular, old titles. They started with unsellable titles in the “back of publishers’ cupboards” says Hertzshtark. “It will take us a little time to grow because we need to start with the edges and grow inside”. Fat Ginger Cat has published around 100 titles on its website and has a couple of hundred monthly subscribers, which “surprised” their partners. Hertshtark believes he can take this chance to ask for more and better content.

GlobalComix decided to work with Western comic publishers to gain reputation before focusing on manga publishers. There are thousands of indie comic creators who are working with GlobalComix and 11 publishers from UK, Canada and US have already set up their own pages on its website. Among those publishers, there is 215Ink which published the first volume of Flutter, one of the best LGBT comics in 2015. He expects that Japanese Company will adapt more slowly to new technology and take more time to finalize a deal than American publishers. However, according to Carter, there are a handful of Japanese publishers who have already heard about GlobalComix on MangaHelpers and have reached out to him first. Other hesitant ones will eventually change, “if their competitors are being successfully internationally by working with us”.
